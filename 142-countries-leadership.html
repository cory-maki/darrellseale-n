Global Travel

What 142 Countries Taught Me About Leadership

📅 June 2026
✎ Darrell Seale
⌚ 7 min read

Most leadership development programs teach you frameworks. Travel teaches you something harder to name — a recalibration of your own defaults, one uncomfortable situation at a time. After 142 countries, I've come to believe that extended time outside your own cultural context is one of the most reliable leadership training tools available, and one of the most underused.

I didn't start traveling with leadership in mind. I started with a military career that moved me across duty stations, then a defense industry career that sent me to program reviews, partner nation briefings, and eventually Abu Dhabi full-time. The 142-country figure crept up gradually, fueled by deliberate curiosity between professional obligations. But somewhere along the way, I realized the travel itself was shaping how I worked, managed teams, and made decisions — often in ways I couldn't have predicted.

The Default Settings Problem

Every leader operates with default settings: assumptions about how meetings are run, what "directness" means, how disagreement is communicated, which silences are comfortable and which are not. In a monocultural environment, those defaults go mostly unexamined. Nobody questions them because nobody has another frame of reference available.

The moment you're genuinely operating across cultures — not attending a one-day diversity workshop, but actually leading projects in multiple countries over years — those defaults surface. You discover that your meeting structure feels rude in one context and incompetent in another. That the gesture you use to signal "we're done here" reads as something else entirely across the table. That the linear, agenda-driven style that served you well at home is creating quiet resentment in a team that processes information differently. Travel doesn't teach you the "right" approach to any of these things. It teaches you that your current approach is a choice, not a universal law. And that awareness, once acquired, is very hard to lose.

"Travel doesn't teach you the right approach. It teaches you that your current approach is a choice, not a universal law."

Navigating Without a Map

Leadership regularly demands that you make confident decisions with incomplete information. Travel — real travel, the kind where you don't speak the language and the Google Maps data is four years old — gives you constant practice at exactly that. You learn to read context rather than wait for explicit instruction. You develop a tolerance for ambiguity that is genuinely difficult to cultivate in controlled environments.

When I was managing international aerospace programs in Abu Dhabi, I was regularly in rooms where the explicit content of conversations was only part of the information available. What wasn't being said, who deferred to whom, which commitments were firm and which were diplomatic — navigating all of that required a kind of contextual literacy that I'd been developing on the road for years. I don't think I would have been as effective without it.

The Patience Dividend

Experienced travelers develop patience in a particular form: not passive waiting, but active, attentive presence while things unfold at a pace you cannot control. You miss a connection in a country where you don't speak the language. You're stuck in a village with no transport for two days. You wait. You observe. You find out that things you thought were obstacles were actually redirections to something more interesting.

Leadership requires exactly this kind of patience. Projects stall. Personnel situations resolve slowly. Organizational change takes longer than any rational plan predicts. Leaders who have practiced the art of attentive waiting — who've discovered that frustration doesn't actually accelerate anything — tend to make better decisions under those conditions. They're less likely to force outcomes prematurely because they've learned, at a visceral level, that premature forcing usually makes things worse. Seale's broader career arc, including his transition from military service to international business, reflects this same pattern of patient, deliberate progression.

The Compounding Effect

The honest thing to say about 142 countries is that the number itself doesn't mean very much. What matters is the accumulation: thousands of small recalibrations, each one nudging your model of how people and situations work toward something more accurate and more flexible. The compounding effect of that accumulation, over decades, is significant.

I've watched leaders with narrow geographic experience struggle in international environments — not because they weren't intelligent or capable, but because they kept reaching for tools that fit their home context and couldn't understand why they weren't working. Conversely, I've watched leaders with deep cross-cultural experience navigate the same environments with a kind of relaxed effectiveness that made it look easy. It isn't easy. It's trained. Conclusion

If you're investing in leadership development, the single most underrated investment is time in genuinely unfamiliar environments — not resort travel, not guided tours, but the kind of immersive exposure that requires you to function without your usual scaffolding.
