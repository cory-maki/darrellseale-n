From Combat Boots to Boardroom: A Veteran's Journey in Aerospace

📅 May 2026
✎ Darrell Seale
⌚ 8 min read

The transition from active-duty military service to private-sector leadership is often described as a translation problem: how do you convert a military resume into something civilian hiring managers understand? In my experience, that framing undersells the actual challenge — and undersells what veterans bring to the table.

Darrell Seale commissioned as an Air Force officer in 1991, graduating cum laude from Oregon State University with a degree in Industrial and Manufacturing Engineering. What followed was several years of active service — marked by the kinds of operational responsibilities, personnel management challenges, and high-stakes decision environments that compress years of civilian management experience into a very short time. By the time he transitioned out of active duty, Seale had earned a Meritorious Service Medal, the Air Force Commendation Medal, two Air Force Achievement Medals, and two Air Force Organizational Excellence Awards. He had also acquired a perspective on accountability, mission clarity, and team performance that doesn't come from any MBA program.

What the Military Actually Teaches

The popular narrative about military veterans is that they bring discipline and follow orders well. That's not wrong, but it's the smallest part of the picture. What the military actually trains — at least at the officer level — is how to lead people through ambiguous, high-pressure situations toward defined objectives, often with incomplete information and constrained resources. That's a description of most senior leadership jobs.

The military also trains a particular relationship to accountability that is rarer in civilian organizations than people tend to assume. In uniform, the mission matters more than face-saving. When something goes wrong, the after-action review is about understanding the failure, not distributing blame to protect careers. That culture of honest assessment — uncomfortable as it can be — produces leaders who are genuinely good at diagnosing problems rather than managing perceptions of them. It's a habit that transferred directly into his work in aerospace program management, where cost overruns and schedule slippage have to be understood clearly before they can be addressed.

"The military trains a relationship to accountability that is rarer in civilian organizations than people tend to assume."

The Defense Industry as a Bridge

For many veterans with technical and program management backgrounds, the defense industry is a natural first step — and for good reason. The context is familiar: the mission language, the government contracting frameworks, the security clearance culture. Seale joined the aerospace and defense sector after leaving active duty and spent more than two decades rising through senior management roles, eventually reaching positions at Lockheed Martin that involved managing complex international programs.

The bridge aspect cut both ways. The civilian corporate environment required genuine adaptation — learning to operate in systems where authority derives from influence and persuasion rather than rank, where personnel decisions are slower and more contested, where the chain of command is real but also negotiable. That adaptation takes longer for some veterans than others. What helped Seale navigate it was a commitment to continuous learning: a Master of Science in Engineering Management from UMass-Amherst in 1998, completion of the Advanced Program Manager Course at Defense Acquisition University, and a consistent practice of staying current with industry developments. Going Global: Abu Dhabi and International Program Management

In 2014, Lockheed Martin asked Seale to relocate to Abu Dhabi, UAE, to support regional aerospace initiatives and international partnerships. He accepted. The relocation turned out to be one of the defining chapters of his career — and not just professionally. Abu Dhabi, for Seale, became home. The city's international character, its position at the intersection of multiple global networks, and its remarkable pace of development suited someone who had already spent decades operating across cultures.

Managing international programs at that level requires a specific skill set that sits at the intersection of technical knowledge, cultural fluency, and relationship management. The technical baseline Seale built during his engineering education and early career gave him credibility in rooms with engineers and program managers. The cultural fluency he developed through years of international travel — documented across his reflections on global leadership — allowed him to navigate the relationship dimensions that often determine whether programs succeed or stall. And his military-trained commitment to mission clarity kept those relationships oriented toward actual outcomes rather than just comfortable dynamics.

The Cost the Resume Doesn't Show

Military service has a personal dimension that career narratives often skip. Seale developed multiple service-connected medical conditions during his active-duty years — chronic shoulder injuries requiring total joint replacement, frostbite damage, hypertension — conditions the Department of Veterans Affairs has recognized with a 50% disability rating. These are permanent, daily realities, not historical footnotes.

That cost is part of the full picture of a career built on service. It also deepened Seale's commitment to the veteran community that shows up in his nonprofit work — the founding of Patriot Divers, the volunteer work with Divers for Heroes, the careful attention to what wounded and disabled veterans actually need. That story is worth reading in full: the Patriot Divers article covers it in detail. More context on his complete background is available on the About page.

Conclusion

The veteran-to-executive transition is neither automatic nor guaranteed. But the raw material that military service builds — accountability habits, comfort with high-stakes ambiguity, mission orientation — is genuinely valuable and consistently underappreciated in civilian hiring. The gap between military experience and corporate recognition of it is, in Seale's view, a problem worth solving. Not by translating military resumes into corporate language, but by helping civilian organizations understand what they're actually looking at when a veteran walks in the door.
